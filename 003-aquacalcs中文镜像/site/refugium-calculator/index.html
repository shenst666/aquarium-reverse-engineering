How It Works

The 避难所计算器 determines the ideal size, lighting, flow rate, and macroalgae requirements for a refugium based on your display tank's volume and bioload. A refugium is a separate breeding ground for beneficial microorganisms and macroalgae that removes excess nutrients from your aquarium while producing copepods and amphipods that serve as natural food for fish and coral. Whether you are a beginner setting up your first tank or an experienced hobbyist expanding a multi-tank system, this calculator provides the data-driven guidance needed to avoid costly mistakes and maintain a thriving aquatic environment. The results account for real-world variables that generic rules of thumb overlook, including filtration efficiency, species-specific bioload requirements, seasonal variations in water chemistry, and the complex interactions between multiple tank inhabitants. Common mistakes in aquarium calculations include using outdated reference data from decades-old fishkeeping guides, ignoring the interaction between multiple variables such as temperature and dissolved oxygen, and failing to build in appropriate safety margins for unexpected conditions like power outages or equipment failures. Professional aquarium maintenance services and public aquarium facilities typically use similar calculation methods when designing and maintaining complex aquatic systems, validating the approach used here against real-world industry practice. Regular recalculation is recommended whenever you add new livestock, change equipment, or notice shifts in water parameters, as aquarium conditions are dynamic and what worked last month may need adjustment as fish grow and biological filtration matures.

The Formula

Minimum Refugium Size = Display Tank Volume × 0.1 to 0.2 (10-20% rule); Recommended Size = Display Tank Volume × 0.25 to 0.33 (25-33% rule). Lighting = Refugium Volume (gallons) × 3-5 watts per gallon. Flow Rate = Refugium Volume (gallons) × 5-10 turnover rate per hour (GPH = gallons × 5 to 10). Chaetomorpha needed = 1 pound per 5-10 gallons of refugium volume.

Variables

  • Display Tank Volume — The total water volume of your main aquarium in gallons; this is the baseline for calculating refugium proportions
  • Bioload Level — The amount of fish waste and organic matter produced in your system (light, moderate, or heavy); higher bioload requires larger refugium and stronger nutrient export
  • Primary Purpose — Whether the refugium is primarily for nutrient export, pod production, macroalgae growth, or a combination; this affects sizing and lighting recommendations
  • Lighting (watts) — The total wattage of lights needed to support macroalgae photosynthesis; typically 3-5 watts per gallon for refugiums
  • Flow Rate (GPH) — Gallons per hour circulating through the refugium; typically 5-10 times the refugium volume per hour to ensure adequate nutrient export
  • Chaetomorpha (pounds) — The amount of macroalgae needed to effectively consume excess nitrate and phosphate; typically 1 pound per 5-10 gallons of refugium volume

Worked Example

Let's say you have a 75-gallon saltwater display tank with a moderate bioload and your primary goal is nutrient export. First, calculate minimum refugium size: 75 gallons × 0.1 = 7.5 gallons minimum. For recommended size: 75 gallons × 0.25 = 18.75 gallons (round to 20 gallons). Next, calculate lighting needs: 20 gallons × 4 watts per gallon = 80 watts of lighting. Calculate flow rate: 20 gallons × 7 turnovers per hour = 140 GPH through the refugium. Finally, determine Chaetomorpha amount: 20 gallons ÷ 7 pounds per gallon = approximately 3 pounds of macroalgae. So you'd want a 20-gallon refugium with an 80-watt light, 140 GPH flow, and 3 pounds of Chaetomorpha. In a second scenario, consider a beginner with a small 10-gallon desktop aquarium wanting to keep a single betta fish and a handful of cherry shrimp. With minimal equipment including a basic sponge filter and a small heater, the calculator adjusts for the lower bioload and smaller volume, producing conservative recommendations appropriate for a nano tank setup. The key consideration here is that parameter stability is much harder to maintain in small volumes because the same amount of waste or chemical imbalance has a proportionally larger impact. The calculator accounts for this by recommending more frequent water changes and lower stocking density relative to the tank size compared to larger systems. For a third scenario, imagine an experienced hobbyist with a large 125-gallon community tank featuring oversized canister filtration rated for 200 gallons and a fully planted aquascape with CO2 injection. The calculator applies enhanced capacity modifiers for the superior filtration and significant biological support from the extensive plant mass, which actively consumes ammonia and nitrate. However, it still maintains conservative safety margins that account for the higher complexity and potential failure modes of a large, heavily stocked system, because a filtration failure or CO2 system malfunction in a densely stocked tank can cause rapid parameter crashes.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

When using the 避难所计算器, several common errors can lead to inaccurate results and potentially harmful outcomes for your aquarium inhabitants. First, many hobbyists use juvenile fish sizes rather than adult sizes in their calculations, leading to overstocking as fish grow to maturity within months. Second, users frequently overestimate their filtration capacity by counting the manufacturer's maximum rating rather than the effective filtration rate, which is typically 60 to 80 percent of the stated maximum once media is loaded and flow is established. Third, failing to account for decorations, substrate, and equipment that displace water volume leads to calculations based on more water than actually exists in the tank. Fourth, ignoring the cumulative bioload of bottom feeders, snails, and shrimp because they seem small individually can push a tank past its safe capacity, as these organisms still produce waste and consume oxygen. The most frequent error is using incorrect measurement units — mixing imperial and metric values produces wildly inaccurate results, so always verify units match what each field specifies. Another common mistake is using rough estimates instead of actual measurements, since even small errors can compound significantly in the final result. Many users forget to account for waste, overlap, or safety margins that are standard in equipment-maintenance work — plan for 5-15 percent additional material depending on project complexity. Ignoring local conditions, codes, and regulations is another pitfall, as this calculator provides general estimates that may not reflect area-specific requirements. Finally, treating results as exact figures rather than estimates leads to problems — always get professional assessments for significant decisions.

Practical Tips

  • Start with the recommended size (25-33% of display tank), not the minimum—a larger refugium provides better nutrient export stability and more consistent pod production than a minimal setup
  • Use LED refugium lights rather than older incandescent or fluorescent options; they run cooler, use less electricity, and won't heat your refugium excessively during summer months
  • Install a dedicated return pump for your refugium with proper valve control so you can adjust flow independently; this lets you dial in the perfect flow rate without affecting display tank circulation
  • Harvest Chaetomorpha every 2-4 weeks by removing about 30-50% of the algae mass—this continuous harvesting maximizes nutrient export and prevents the algae from becoming too dense and trapping detritus
  • Include a baffle or overflow mechanism to prevent larger fish or invertebrates from entering the refugium; keep it as a dedicated safe zone for copepods, amphipods, and macroalgae to thrive
  • Document your calculation results and actual outcomes over time to build a personal reference database. Tracking the relationship between calculated values and observed results helps you calibrate future estimates and identify patterns specific to your setup, water source, and maintenance routine.
  • Cross-reference the results from this calculator with at least one other source or method before making significant purchases or changes. No single calculator can account for every variable in your specific situation, and comparing multiple estimates helps identify potential errors or unusual conditions.
  • Consider seasonal variations when interpreting your results. Water temperature, ambient humidity, evaporation rates, and even municipal water chemistry can change significantly between summer and winter, affecting the accuracy of calculations based on a single set of conditions.

Frequently Asked Questions

What's the difference between minimum and recommended refugium size?

The minimum size (10-20% of display volume) is the bare-bones amount needed for basic nutrient export and pod production, but it offers little margin for error and slower nutrient consumption. The recommended size (25-33%) provides significantly better results, more stable water chemistry, higher pod populations, and better long-term nutrient control. Most experienced aquarists choose recommended size to avoid frequent maintenance and achieve better biological stability.

Why does bioload level matter for refugium design?

A heavy bioload system (overstocked, protein-heavy foods, poor filtration) produces more excess nutrients (nitrate and phosphate) that need to be exported. Higher bioload requires a larger refugium, more macroalgae, and stronger flow to effectively consume all the excess waste. Light bioload systems can get away with smaller refugiums because the nutrient load is naturally lower.

Can I use less lighting in my refugium to save electricity?

Yes, but with diminishing returns on nutrient export and pod production. Macroalgae require 12+ hours of light daily and at least 3-4 watts per gallon to photosynthesize efficiently and grow. Using only 1-2 watts per gallon will result in slow algae growth, poor nitrate uptake, and lower copepod reproduction—defeating much of the refugium's purpose. Standard 3-5 watts per gallon is the practical sweet spot.

How often should I harvest Chaetomorpha, and what happens if I don't?

Harvest every 2-4 weeks, removing 30-50% of the algae mass each time. Harvesting is how you actually export nutrients—when you remove the algae, you're physically removing the nitrogen and phosphorus it absorbed. If you never harvest, the algae will continue growing until it becomes dense, traps detritus, and eventually dies and decays, releasing those nutrients back into the system.

What if my refugium is too small to fit the recommended flow rate?

If space is extremely limited, prioritize flow rate over size—a smaller refugium with excellent turnover (10x per hour) will export nutrients more efficiently than a slightly larger one with poor flow. Alternatively, use a sump-style refugium integrated into your existing filtration system, which allows for proper flow without requiring additional tank space in the display area.
